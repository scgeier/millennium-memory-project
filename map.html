Where We Were

Most conversations about a flashbulb memory start with the same question: “Do you remember where you were when...?” Location is crucial to the memory; the little details about your surroundings are usually what give the memory such a peculiar, autobiographical context, and they’re what make the stories so fascinating. Ask a Baby Boomer where she was when Kennedy was shot, and she’ll end up telling you about the color of the trays in her middle school cafeteria.

I wanted to see it on a map, and find out if there was any correlation between the strength of the memories and where those memories were formed.

This map shows where each of the respondents to the Millennium survey lived in 1999. The color of the dots corresponds to the vividness of their memories.

New Year’s Eve in America has long been associated with New York and the ball drop in Times Square, and perhaps not surprisingly, people who were living in the New York area at the turn of the millennium have more vivid memories from that night -- 24 percent more vivid than the average, to be precise. More surprising is the fogginess of millennium memories on the West Coast; people in Los Angeles scored 21 percent lower on the memory survey than the overall average. Small sample size may account for much of the difference (see my Methods and Limitations).

Time zones may have also played a role. During several of my in-person interviews, people remembered watching the New Year’s celebrations on TV in earlier countries around the world to find out if the Y2K bug was wreaking havoc before it hit the United States. When they saw that Australia, for example, was doing fine, they realized it was a non-event, and the rest of the night became a blur. By the time it was midnight on the West Coast, it was old news.
