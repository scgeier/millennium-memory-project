Who We Were With

On the night of December 31, 1999, I was at a house party in Charlotte, North Carolina, with my future wife, her then-best friend (emphasis, unfortunately, on then), some of her friend’s friends, and a bunch of strangers. When the clock struck midnight, I was standing in a hallway outside of some random dude’s bedroom. I remember thinking, “It’s the Year 2000. Shouldn’t I be standing on a mountaintop or something?”

In the years since, I’ve realized that my disappointment in the moment had nothing to do with the ho-hum surroundings; it was because I didn’t know most of the people surrounding me. Once-in-a-lifetime events should be shared with people you love. I had my girlfriend by my side, but we were stuck in a sea of strangers. Thank goodness the computers didn’t crash that night; we would entered the Apocalypse with a bunch of investment bankers with names like Fitz and Carter.

Education Religion Politics

Remembering the people around you is one of the cornerstones of flashbulb memory. So for this project, I wanted to know: Who were we with on New Year’s Eve, 1999? And I wanted to get quantitative data, so I could look for patterns.

For this section of the research, I had to exclude people who were underage at the turn of the millennium, since most of them were at home with their parents and siblings, and this would have skewed the results. And of course, the question had to be focused on people who remember the night well enough to remember who they spent it with. As a result, the pool of responses for this chord diagram was relatively small (n = 71), and the results require a grain or two of salt.

Nonetheless, there were some interesting patterns. Hover over Tap on the columns in the chart to explore.

Only seven percent of respondents were among strangers on New Year's Eve 1999. These people were like me -- they were at a large party, or a bar, or public gathering (or if they were at home, they had a party big enough that some strangers rolled in). Of that group, a disproportionate number --- 55 percent -- have only a high school education. People with a bachelor's degree make up the lion's share of the group that was with their parents (69 percent). Atheists were also more likely to be with a parent that night; they made up only 17 percent of the overall pool but 46 percent of the group that was with mom or dad.

Republicans were more likely to be with their spouses and children (they were only 22 percent of the overall respondents, but more than 40 percent of the spouse and kids connection). And the people out in public or at a big party (i.e. the ones among strangers)? They were overwhelmingly Democrats and Independents.
